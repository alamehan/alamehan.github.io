Introduction to Understanding the User


Fundamentally, every product or design is intended for a user: to address challenges, to make tasks easier, to improve their lives. The first step in creating effective designs is to understand people.

Well-executed user research helps designers see past our assumptions and find creative solutions. In many cases, you’ll be designing products for users who are different from yourself. Even if you are among your product’s intended users, user research can help broaden your perspective.

Design methodologies like design thinking, user-centered design, the double diamond diagram, and the product development life cycle may have different terms for the discovery phase of the design process, but they all emphasize understanding and empathizing with the user.

Engaging in user research helps designers and companies:

  • Develop products people actually need
  • Learn what users care about and what motivates them
  • Find gaps and pain points in the market or competitive landscape
  • Define exactly which problem to solve
  • Hone in on target users to determine who is most likely to benefit from a solution
  • Decide whether a product idea is actually adding anything useful

In this lesson, we’ll walk through how to get started with defining a problem and forming a research question, how to choose between user research methods during the early stages of the design process, and how to start planning out your research. Later on, we’ll dive even deeper into specific methods and sharing your research results.

Generative User Research

Imagine you’re a designer on a team starting the design process for a new product in the travel space. You have decided to focus on building a platform for young adults.

As we mentioned in the last exercise, you’ll often find yourself designing a product for users who aren’t just like you. Even if you’re designing to solve a problem you’ve experienced, it’s useful to examine the broader landscape and explore how others think about it.

For example, some of us might be young adults right now. Others were young adults at some point in our lives, or maybe some of us are parents of young adults. From any of those perspectives, we might assume that we already understand what young adults would seek in a travel product and what challenges they face when traveling. But before you embark on designing a solution, you need to get to know your actual users and figure out what their real motivations and pain points are.

Generative user research, also known as exploratory, discovery, or foundational research, occurs during the earlier stages of product development and focuses on discovering the motivations and pain points experienced by users. These types of user research work best during the early stages of product development, when you’re trying to get to know your user base.

Some types of generative user research include:

  • Interviews: A researcher asks each participant a series of questions about a topic.
  • Surveys: A broad range of users are sent the same set of questions to collect broad insights and trends about a given topic or market.
  • Diary studies: Longitudinal, or longer-term, qualitative data is collected from participants by asking them to complete entries in a diary which may include a series of prompts.
  • Ethnography and field studies: These methods observe behavior in a natural setting and often occur over time, offering more long-term or continuous insight into participant behavior.

In contrast to generative user research, evaluative user research, such as user testing, evaluates or assesses an existing design or prototype so the design team can improve and iterate.

Defining the Problem Space

Often when we think about product development, we think about designing the best solution or product. Early-stage user research is also about figuring out exactly which problem needs to be solved.

When you’re starting the product development process, consider the problem space and the scope of the problem you’re trying to address. For example, for our travel product, the goal of “transforming the travel industry” would be too broad of a scope for a single project. “Helping young adults plan a trip” would be a more reasonable scope and help you form tangible research questions to find problems you can help solve.

When selecting a topic for user research, consider the following:

  • What are you trying to learn?
  • What is your focused research goal?
  • What are some research question(s) to help achieve your goal?
  • Which research method(s) will help answer your research question(s)? (More on this later.)
  • Who do you want to study or include in your research? (More on this later.)

Thinking about our travel example, you might ask, “How are young adults planning travel today?” and explore the motivations behind how they are putting together an itinerary and which parts of the process feel most difficult. The results of your research can help you identify a real problem, develop a solution that is actually useful, and deliver it to the right audience.

You might discover through user research that the problem you suspected is not actually the core issue. For example, the users you speak to may have no trouble planning a solo travel itinerary, but struggle to coordinate or share with friends and family. Or you might discover that users visiting urban destinations do not seem to have a particular problem, but users visiting more rural destinations do.

In planning your research, consider what stage you are at in the design process through a methodology like the double diamond diagram. During the convergent strategy phase of this methodology, broader generative research is analyzed and the team moves forward toward problem definition.

As we walk through more research methods in this lesson, consider how we might eventually move from divergent thinking to convergent thinking about the problem to be solved, and which methods would best help define the problem for a given research project.

You’ve decided to design a new product related to music. Think of a well-scoped research question that you could begin to look into.
Some well-scoped examples of research questions could be:

  • How do people listen to music at work?
  • How do people find concerts to attend while traveling?
  • How do millennials find music related to music they already like?
  • How can we design a product to help teenagers learn music theory?

It’s important to keep your research question focused and specific. Examples of overly broad or vague research questions on this topic could be:

  • How do people relate to music?
  • How can we design a product to transform the music space?

Now that you have an overarching research question, think about who you’d want to start speaking to and what research method you’d want to use. Don’t worry about diving too deep into this right now; we’ll cover more specifics on these topics later.
For the “How can we design a product to help teenagers learn music theory?” question above, you might consider speaking to teenagers, or to people like teachers and parents of teenagers who are aware of their habits or may make purchasing decisions on their behalf.

Interviews or surveys could be a good way to start getting a sense of their self-reported behaviors and motivations, or a diary study could help give you a better sense for patterns over time.

Quantitative Research

Quantitative research methods are those that collect numerically measurable data. These methods allow UX professionals to discover broad patterns, compare different designs and their trade-offs, and tie their work back to company goals and key performance indicators (KPIs).

Compared to qualitative research, which we’ll cover in the next exercise, quantitative research generally provides high level data about a larger number of participants.

Some popular quantitative research methods include:

  • Surveys or questionnaires: A broad range of users are sent the same set of questions to collect broad insights and trends about a given topic or market.
  • Card sorting: Participants organize topics into groups to help uncover an information architecture that aligns with users’ mental models.
  • Web analytics: Web analytics, including A/B testing and heat maps, are usually used in the later evaluative stages of user research but can also feed into discovery in an iterative design process.

Quantitative methods can be used at any stage of the design process, but are often used at the beginning and end, first to gain an understanding of the market or ecosystem, and later to evaluate the performance of a product or design.

Qualitative Research

Qualitative research methods examine why users behave the way they do in depth, and focus on the motivations and thought processes behind a user’s experience.

Compared to quantitative research, covered in the previous exercise, qualitative research generally provides richer data about a smaller number of participants. Quantitative research can help build empathy with users and provide illustrative anecdotes.

Some popular qualitative research methods include:

  • Interviews: A researcher asks each participant a structured, unstructured, or semi-structured series of questions about a topic.
  • Focus groups: A researcher guides a group of 3–10 participants through a series of questions about a topic, product, or design.
  • Diary studies: Longitudinal qualitative data is collected from participants by asking them to complete entries in a diary which may include a series of prompts.
  • Ethnography and field studies: These methods observe user behavior in a natural setting and often occur over time, offering more long-term or continuous insight into participant behavior.
  • Contextual inquiry: Researchers conduct an interview in context as participants complete tasks.

Qualitative methods can be used at any stage of the design process, but may provide especially useful insights during the discovery and ideation stages, when teams are trying to get to know target users in-depth

Working With Mixed Methods

Good user research works with a mixture of methods to collect insights about both the bigger picture and the details. While some designers or researchers eventually specialize in particular methods, you’ll still want to be familiar with a mixture of qualitative, quantitative, behavioral, and attitudinal research methods.

Combining qualitative methods with quantitative methods can help explain why users are behaving in a particular way. Combining quantitative methods with qualitative methods can help explain the broad patterns behind user behavior. For example, interviewing users (qualitative) after a survey (quantitative) can offer an understanding of the motivations behind trends or why outliers answered differently.

It’s also important to combine attitudinal research with behavioral research for a full understanding of user behavior.

Attitudinal research methods focus on self-reported thoughts, beliefs, and needs from users, or put simply, what users say, rather than what they do. These methods include:

  • Interviews
  • Focus groups
  • Diary studies
  • Surveys

Behavioral research methods, on the other hand, directly observe user behavior.

  • Ethnography and field studies
  • Web analytics
  • Tree testing
  • User testing

Anthropologist Margaret Mead once said, “What people say, what people do, and what people say they do are entirely different things.” Combining these methods helps us understand both what users believe and how they act in practice. You might consider combining diary studies or interviews (attitudinal) with field studies or observation (behavioral) to get a sense for how users report their own behavior, as opposed to what they do in practice.

Mixing research methods doesn’t just apply to collecting your research data, but also how you might analyze it. Sometimes this is referred to as triangulation, “the practice of using multiple sources of data or multiple approaches to analyzing data, to enhance the credibility of a research study.”

A research project including both interviews and ethnography combines which types of research methods?
Interviews are considered a qualitative, attitudinal research method, while ethnography is considered a qualitative, behavioral research method. These methods would be considered complementary because they combine attitudinal and behavioral research.

A research project including both a survey and focus groups combines which types of research methods?
Surveys are considered a quantitative, attitudinal research method, while focus groups are considered a qualitative, attitudinal research method. These methods would be considered complementary because they combine quantitative and qualitative research.

Creating a Research Plan

Once you’ve chosen the methods you want to use for your user research, you can begin preparing and executing your research. The first step in the process is always to write a research plan. If you’re working with a team, this ensures everyone is on the same page. If you’re working solo, it gives you a document to return to and adds intentionality to the research process.

A research plan should cover:

Background: Why are you embarking on this research?

Research goal: What are you hoping to uncover with this research?

Research question(s): What question or questions will help you find answers that help you achieve your research goal?

Research method(s) and protocols: What research method or methods will you use? How will you specifically execute them?

For example, if you’re conducting interviews, consider how many interviews you’d like to conduct, whether you’ll conduct them in-person or remotely, the target duration of the interviews, and how you’ll structure them. For a survey, you might consider how many participants you’d like to include, what survey platform you’ll be using, and how long the survey will run. You may also want to include plans for how you will analyze your results.

Target participants: Who would you ideally like to talk to or include in your research?

  • Do you have existing customers you can reach out to? Is it better if you speak with people unfamiliar with your product? Or a mix of both?
  • What demographics are you hoping to reach?
  • Is there anywhere that they gather online or offline that you can recruit them?


In many cases, you will create a participant screener, a short survey or to qualify participants.

Recruitment strategies: Where will you seek out participants? For example, you might consider using an existing customer list or reaching out in online communities. Consider whether you will offer any compensation or incentives to participants. Be upfront with participants about time and effort required and how they will be compensated.

Timeline and due dates: How long will each stage of the research take and when do you expect the project to be completed? A realistic timeline and scope will help you set appropriate expectations and plan your work.

Any ethical considerations: How will you protect your participants and what can you realistically promise to them?

For sensitive topics, you may want to take participant privacy into special consideration. Consider informed consent, and let participants know ahead of participation what risks the research may entail and how their contributions will be used.

Research materials to be created: Think about what research, recruitment, and reporting collateral your team will need to create. This could include any informed consent forms or explanatory materials, interview scripts, survey questions, or anything else you might want to create in advance.

It can be useful to pre-write e-mail communication and plan for what communication will look like from start to finish, to ensure quick follow-ups and consistency across participants.

Considering Diversity and Inclusion

Imagine you want to learn about the pain points of commuting to work through a survey. If you only survey people living in major cities, the results might be “subway and buses are packed.” If you only survey people living in suburbs, the results might be related to traffic jams. In conducting user research, you should be intentional about who is included, and who might be left out. Considering diversity and inclusion early in the research process will lead to more inclusive designs and products down the line.

Consider various users who may use the product and how and when they can be involved in the design process. In some cases, this may mean being humble and admitting that it may be best to empower communities or groups to solve their own problems, rather than designing on their behalf.

User research can help give the people you are designing for more agency, and there are many different ways of thinking about how and when to involve users:

Accessibility: Accessibility refers to designing devices, products, and environments such that individuals with disabilities or sensory impairments can successfully use them. The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) break down accessibility into four main principles: perceivable, operable, understandable, and robust.

Inclusive Design: Inclusive design is a discipline of design that emphasizes designing products and services that work for people of all demographics, perspectives, and abilities, with a focus on those who may have been historically marginalized or excluded.

Universal Design: Universal design focuses on creating one experience that can include as many people as possible. The principles of universal design consider how designs can be useful to people of diverse abilities, or adapt flexibly to users of different preferences and abilities.

User-Centered Design (UCD): User-centered design is a design methodology consisting of an iterative process that puts users at the center of product development and involves them in the design from the beginning. UCD processes generally involve the following activities: understand, specify, design, and evaluate.

Further Examples: Some further examples of inclusive design methodologies include:

  • Design justice: This approach “rethinks design processes, centers people who are normally marginalized by design, and uses collaborative, creative practices to address the deepest challenges our communities face.”
  • Value-sensitive design: This approach to design aims to center human values and ethics in the design process and acknowledges the potential trade-offs and potential unintentional results tied to our design choices.
  • Participatory design: Also known as co-design, this design approach aims to involve users or stakeholders in actively designing solutions for themselves.

Sharing Your Research

You’ve completed a round of rigorous research. Now, how do you get the rest of your team on board? In UX design and research roles, part of your responsibility is to advocate for the user and share research findings across your organization.

Once you’ve analyzed your research, you’re ready to share it in a research report of your findings and recommendations.

Sharing your research is a process of storytelling. You can apply a design mindset to your research report and your methods for sharing your findings: think about the audience you’re presenting to and what kind of format and messaging is likely to resonate with them.

For busy C-suite types, a brief executive summary of key takeaways might be all they have a chance to read. You might consider pulling the most relevant takeaways for different teams: different points might be relevant to your colleagues in engineering and product compared to your colleagues in marketing and sales.

You might consider including other teammates in the analysis process to make them feel like part of the process, make research results memorable, and actively build empathy for the user on the team. For example, you might organize an affinity mapping session with sticky notes or an online whiteboarding tool. We suggest Miro for this, given it’s wide array of features and ease-of-use, but some other tools include FigJam and Jamboard.

Consider using multiple formats to make your research more memorable and get it across to people with different learning styles. For example, you might present at a team meeting, create a written report, send a summary email, or even make a video. Get creative and think about how you can best advocate for the user. Make sure that research findings are well-organized and documented so your team can easily reference them in the future and continue building on existing research.

Based on research findings, researchers can also create personas, user journey maps, and storyboards to share within an organization:

  • Personas: Fictional archetypes of the target users of a product or design, created as composites of user research or market research.
  • User journey maps: A timeline of user actions related to accomplishing a particular goal related to a product or design.
  • Storyboards: A sequence of panels (often resembling a comic book) depicting a user journey chronologically.

👉 How to Conduct User Interviews

Learn how to prepare for and conduct successful user interviews.

Imagine this scenario… Your team has found a need in the travel community - finding cool things to do in a new place is hard! Especially if you want to avoid the tourist traps! Planning a vacation takes time and effort, making it feel like the job you’re escaping from. Your team wants to build a platform to allow different people to share itineraries and recommendations when traveling to different cities or countries - to streamline the process of planning for a vacation.

Should this be a mobile app, a website, or a physical magazine? Should it be forum-based or recommendation-based? How will users search for cities or itineraries? How will people be incentivized to share? Will people actually use this? There are a lot of questions to consider!

Luckily, there is a defined target audience—people who like to travel! But it’s such a large and varied group of people that it would be helpful to get more specific information about potential users.

As designers, we often think of target audiences as groups and users as average members of these groups. But within these audiences, individual users have unique thoughts, feelings, and ideas. So how do we gain insight into some of this personal feedback?

In this article, we’ll discuss an effective method to empathize with individual users and learn more about the target audience as a whole - by conducting user interviews!

What is a User Interview?

User interviews are a UX research method where a researcher conducts interviews with individual participants. User interviews are used to better understand what a given user thinks about a design, process, or product.

User interviews can be used in many different design stages. They can be conducted at the beginning of the design process - to develop more accurate personas or journey maps. Or maybe, after an initial mockup is created - to learn more about the perceptions and feelings toward a design. Or instead, following a usability test, a method where users will perform a series of tests to evaluate the product, a user interview can be used to learn about the user’s thought process while doing the tasks and compare the verbal response to observed behaviors. User interviews can be used in many other scenarios!

The ability of user interviews to be effective in many different phases of the design process allows it to be a useful tool for practically any project or product. But regardless of the phase it is being used in, to conduct an effective user interview you first need to define a clear and concrete goal.

Types of User Interviews

There are three types of user interviews: unstructured, semi-structured, and structured.

Unstructured interviews are defined by a few open-ended questions. There isn’t a specific outline to guide you - rather, the user’s responses lead the way. This format allows the conversation to flow naturally, putting the user more at ease, and you might uncover topics you wouldn’t have thought of prior to the interview. However, because every interview might be starkly different, there is much less control between participants, making it difficult to compare answers across different interview sessions.

Semi-structured interviews allow the researcher to adapt the interview based on the participant, but also have a general plan or outline that is created prior to the interview to lead the conversation towards a certain goal. The researcher will develop a series of questions and topics that will remain constant across participants, but then also leave time for follow-up questions that arise naturally based on different responses. The researcher might also probe the user to complete certain interactions and then ask further questions about their thought process while completing the tasks. This method still allows the researcher to ensure relevant insight, but also have some semblance of control among participants. While there still is a risk of introducing uncontrolled bias, the balance between flexibility and control makes this type of interview the most commonly conducted for UX projects.

Structured interviews have a very clear agenda and outline that is defined prior to the interview. Every participant is asked the same questions and follow-up questions - regardless of their answers. This method does allow the researcher to control the types of insight they will receive, as well as limit the overall bias. This method will produce the most controlled, reliable qualitative data. However, due to the structure, there is little room for additional explanation or uncovered topics and ideas, so the insight might be limited.

Once you have a goal and you know what type of interview you want to conduct, what’s next? How do you actually prepare and conduct a user interview?

Planning Your Research

Before we can conduct our user interview, we first need to develop a research plan. Writing a research plan is vital to ensure your user research is well-oriented and the research goal is clearly communicated to your team.

↪ Defining a Goal

An established, concrete research goal related to specific aspects of user behaviors or perspectives can help you develop relevant interview questions and lead the interview more effectively.

To develop a goal, answer the following questions:

  • What stage of the design process are you at?
  • What are you hoping to learn about from the interview?
  • What questions do stakeholders have?
  • Do you have a mockup or prototype that you want the user to give feedback on?

Your goal should be specific and detailed enough to provide structure to your interview questions. When developing your goal, it is important to outline a specific target group, as well as the specific task, process, or product that you will ask these users about. A successful goal outlines the information that the researcher is hoping to learn and provides a framework to develop the interview questions.

The following assessment will test your ability to evaluate an interview goal.

Once you have defined the goal, it’s time to decide what type of user interview you should conduct! Let’s take a look at the different types of user interviews and how to pick one that aligns best with the research goal.

↪ Preparing for a User Interview

After defining a goal and choosing which type of interview you will conduct, you will want to do some prep work. Before conducting the interview, you need to recruit your participants, prepare a discussion guide, write interview questions, and set up the interview environment.

  1. Recruiting Participants: First things first - you need some users! Keep in mind - the goal here is to find people that represent the audiences you are trying to reach. You will need to decide how many users you want to interview, what target audiences will be represented, and how you will recruit these participants!

While each project will be slightly different, it can be helpful to build off of the principle established by Jakob Nielson - and start with five participants. If you notice that the interviews are getting repetitive or your resources are scarce, scale down to fewer. If you are looking to learn about many target audiences or you have abundant resources, scale-up and interview a few more.

Once you have defined what you’re looking for, there are many ways to find participants. You can use techniques such as guerrilla testing, past user databases, online recruitment platforms, or hiring a third-party recruitment service.

  1. Prepare a Discussion Guide: A discussion guide is a document that outlines the questions and topics you want to cover during the interview, as well as a general timeline for the interview. It should also include a script to direct you on how to instruct the user. Depending on the type of interview you have chosen, the discussion guide may be extremely detailed and strict - or open-ended and flexible.

You should test your discussion guide prior to the interview to establish timing and instruction clarity, as well as help the interview flow more naturally.

  1. Writing Interview Questions: And now for the real bread and butter of the interview - the questions! It’s important to write questions that lead users to open up about the topics you are interested in learning about - without prompting them to answer in a certain way.

To do this, it’s important to ask open and non-leading questions. An open question is a question that cannot be answered with a “yes” or “no” - requiring a user to go into more detail. A leading question is a question that hints at a specific answer - leading the user to respond in a different way.

You will also want to try to anticipate different answers and construct follow-up questions based on the goal. You might also want to prepare additional or backup questions.

  1. Setting Up the Environment & Making the User Feel Comfortable: People are influenced by their physical environment. Ensuring a calm and positive environment will help your participants feel relaxed and focused on the task. Think about the convenience of your location for the user and how the location might lead to biased answers.

    DO’S
  • ✅ DO Try to pick a neutral and quiet location.
  • ✅ DO Allow plenty of time for you and the user to get settled before the interview.
  • ✅ DO Create rapport with the user. Get to know them a little bit and share a bit about yourself. Explain the purpose of the interview and allow the user to ask any questions about the process.

DON’TS

  • ❌ DON’T Pick a location that is inconvenient for the user to get to. A user who just sat in 45 minutes of traffic may not be in the best mood for an interview.
  • ❌ DON’T Rush. Schedule enough time to find the location, introduce yourself, answer questions, go to the bathroom, or drink some water - all before the interview even starts.
  • ❌ DON’T Treat the interview like an interrogation. It should feel like a natural conversation between you and the user.

Benefits and Drawbacks

User interviews can be an incredibly helpful tool for many products and processes, allowing users to provide active feedback as the design moves through iterations. They allow you to ask specific questions about information that would be difficult to receive through other research methods.

However, because the data collected is self-reported, it’s important to keep in mind the limitations that come with it. For reasons that don’t pertain to the design, the user response may be inaccurate or misleading. A user may misremember information, leave out details that would be important, or change their responses to portray a certain self-image. The tendency to consciously or subconsciously provide inaccurate information due to the nature of a self-reporting method is called response bias. There are many different categories of response bias, each with different methods to limit the effects.

Acquiescence bias is the tendency to agree with the interviewer. In general, users are more likely to agree than to disagree. To avoid this type of bias, avoid asking questions that imply there is a “correct” answer. Instead of asking for agreement or disagreement to a predefined statement, ask the user to provide a statement that reflects their point of view.

Try rewriting this question to avoid acquiescence bias.

Would you agree that you are satisfied with the current interface?

Demand characteristics bias is a change in how a user answers questions due to the knowledge that they are part of a research study. Social desirability bias is a tendency to provide answers that align with characteristics or viewpoints that the user views as socially desirable. It is difficult to completely avoid these types of biases, as a user will always know they are being interviewed, but you can minimize the effects by providing a comfortable environment, creating rapport and engaging in natural conversation throughout the interview, and keeping the user’s personal details anonymous.

Interview bias is due to a user’s perception of the interviewer. This may be due to a user’s own biased perception of an interviewer’s ethnicity, age, gender, or physical appearance - or even details like body language or facial expressions. Interview bias can be difficult to avoid - so it is just important to keep in mind when evaluating the data.

Conclusion

Despite limitations and biases, user interviews are an extremely effective method to truly empathize with the user and understand their thoughts, feelings, and perspective. By preparing effectively, adhering to good interview practices, and avoiding biases as much as possible, you can conduct a highly successful interview that may be the key to a design that is truly user-centric.

👉 How to Analyze Qualitative Data

Learn how to apply a variety of analysis methods on the qualitative data collected in user research studies.

You’ve recruited participants, prepared research materials, and successfully conducted your first user interview study! So, now what? During your user interview, you likely collected a lot of valuable qualitative data! What do you do with the data you have collected? What does it tell you about your users? What does this mean for your product? And once you figure out what it means, how do you actually use those findings to improve your design?

Many user research methods are used to gather qualitative data - which can seem daunting or confusing to analyze. But it doesn’t have to be overwhelming! Understanding how to organize your data and apply effective methods and techniques will allow you to develop research findings and answer questions about your design.

What is Qualitative Data?

Qualitative user data is any non-numerical data collected in a user research study. These data are the thoughts, behaviors, opinions, perceptions, or feelings of the user that are recorded through the research method. For example, qualitative user data can be obtained through user interview transcripts, observations the researcher makes of how the participant interacts with an interface, or user survey responses.

Qualitative data is important in user research because it is a direct insight into users’ thoughts, feelings, and perceptions - information that is difficult to gather using numerical values. Whereas quantitative data is often analyzed to discover similarities or patterns across data sets, qualitative data is used to understand how these patterns arise, why users act the way that they do, and how to change the design to account for user behavior. For example, through a quantitative analysis, we might discover that users do indeed spend the most time on the homepage of a website, compared to any other page. Through qualitative analysis, we might learn that the reason that users spend the most time on this page is actually because the navigation menu is difficult to find and they are initially unsure how to navigate to the other pages. While without quantitative analysis we would not have a pattern of behavior to look more deeply into, without qualitative analysis we would not understand how to make the correct design choices to respond to this behavior. To learn more about why qualitative data is important in UX research, read Data Is More than Numbers: Why Qualitative Data Isn’t Just Opinions by the Nielsen Norman group.

Yet, as useful as this type of user data is, conducting qualitative analysis can be confusing or daunting. There can be a lot of data that is recorded in just one qualitative user study - lengthy, messy data. And, it’s not quite as straightforward as quantitative methods.

So before jumping into any analysis method, it’s important to gather all of your data and effectively organize it in a way that will make the qualitative analysis process more manageable.

Organizing User Data from User Research

First, gather all your data and convert them all into a common digital format. Interview recordings can be transcribed, photographs described and uploaded, and usability notes gathered and labeled.

Then, categorize your data based on participants. For example, if you have conducted a user interview, you could make a separate folder for each participant’s interview transcript. To that folder, you might want to add a document of information about the interview location, length, discussion guides, technical difficulties or pain points, or any other notes the researcher took during the interview. Additionally, it would be helpful to include a document describing participant details, such as name, age, relevant characteristics, or anything else that might be helpful in understanding the user.

Finally, label your data accordingly to make it easy to find, access, and keep track of these data points. Refer back to the research objectives and add any additional notes that might make it easier throughout the analysis process. Returning to the user interviews example, you may want to make an additional document that outlines your research goals and project design requirements, to easily reference as you look at the data itself and pick out the useful information.

Once your data is effectively organized, there are a variety of analysis methods you can apply to better understand what this data actually means for the user and design process.

Methods to Analysis

There is no one size fits all for qualitative analysis methods - so understanding a number of them will help you apply the right one for each unique data set. In many cases, it might be helpful to use more than one and see what sort of findings are uncovered. But at the core of this research stage, these methods are designed to help you uncover relevant themes, patterns, and stories represented in the data.

Thematic analysis is a system of analysis with the purpose of identifying patterns to uncover relevant themes. A theme is an idea, need, or pattern of meaning that presents itself repeatedly in a data set.

↪ Coding Data

Coding data is a thematic analysis tool in which segments of text are labeled using codes - to easily identify, compare, and sort these points of data. A code is a word or phrase that represents a segment of text, acting as a shorthand for the description of the information. This is not writing code in a programming language - no need for variables or functions here!

When building up coded data, it is helpful to have a reference to the description of each code and examples of what type of data is related. This reference is helpful especially when working in teams or with data over a long period of time, to ensure that the researcher understands and uses the codes correctly.

Once the data is coded, you can use these codes to group, compare or contrast the data segments. Codes make it easy to parse through a large amount of qualitative data and quickly sort through them, discover relationships, and develop themes.

There are many different types and methods of coding, such as in vivo coding, process coding, open coding, descriptive coding, structural coding, and value coding.

↪ Journaling

Journaling is a method where a researcher will record their thought process about a set of qualitative data. To do so, a researcher will annotate research materials, transcripts, or collected data as well as write down memos. Memos are notes the researcher makes on their ideas, thought processes, or reflections.

Journaling can be an effective way to develop themes, as well as a way to record the process of arriving at those themes.

↪ Affinity Mapping

Affinity mapping is an analysis method in which a researcher pulls important points from a data set and categorizes them visually, creating a map of the data. Affinity mapping is useful to better sort and understand your data, as well as discover common themes that groups of data points might fall into.

The first step of creating an affinity map is to parse through all the data and pull out the important points. These points might be quotes, observations of interests, common topics, etc. This process is very similar to the process of coding data. Each point should be written on an individual sticky note - either physically or digitally.

An effective affinity map note is clear, specific, and provides enough detail to quickly remind the researcher of the overarching point and relevance. The data point should also stand alone, in order to be best organized within groups and themes. If you are mapping data from a number of users or sources, it is also helpful to note which participant or source the data point comes from. If it is a specific quote from a user, also make sure to note that as well, either with quotation marks, formatting differences, or labels.

Next, sort through the notes and start to group them based on their similarities, creating a visual cluster of notes that share a theme or particular meaning. Once all relevant notes are organized into respective clusters, decide on a label for the cluster that represents the theme those notes convey.

Finally, discuss the categories in reference to the research objectives, noting the priority of necessary clusters or individual notes. This will help you understand how the findings from the affinity map will be reflected in the next phase of the design process.

Affinity mapping can be an effective tool in a variety of user research studies, but it’s important to understand the advantages and disadvantages to be able to apply it at a constructive time.

The benefits of the method are that it is highly collaborative, quick, cheap, and easy to conduct. It supports an iterative dynamic, which is crucial when using a design-thinking process. Finally, the visual nature makes it easy for a team to gain a shared understanding, summarize the data, develop themes, and have a good reference for the rest of the process.

On the other hand, in situations where there is a lot of data or the data is extremely varied, affinity mapping may not be the strongest choice of analysis method. It may take an excessive amount of time to parse through it all or there may be too many categories to develop strong themes or meanings.

To learn more on how to apply some of these analysis methods, take a look at the Nielsen Norman Group article on How to Analyze Qualitative Data from UX Research: Thematic Analysis.

Developing Findings & Reporting Insights

Once you have organized, analyzed, and found themes, it is time to synthesize the data to develop findings and insights. Beginning with the high priority items, think about the main takeaways of each thematic category, how this information meets your research goal, and how this finding can impact the design. Once you have identified these takeaways and points, you can build them into research findings to share with stakeholders, designers, and other members of the team.

A good framework for sharing a research finding is outlining an insight and recommendation. An insight is a pattern of observations made by the researcher based on the data. A recommendation describes how to solve or improve upon the observation the insight describes.

For example, let’s say a theme that was uncovered through an affinity map is organization lists. The researchers observe that a number of the users they interviewed use lists or other category systems when planning for travel - and that they seem to be satisfied with these systems! The insight might be “Users gravitate towards using lists and category systems to organize travel plans”. Then the researchers will think about what they can or should keep or change about their design based on this insight. The recommendation might be “Allow users to easily create and access lists throughout the planning process”. The complete finding would be “Users gravitate towards using lists and category systems to organize travel plans, therefore users should be able to easily create and access lists throughout the planning process.” Simple enough!

Because users tend to create a budget in the early stages of travel planning, users should have easy access to travel plan expenditures and a way to compare it to a given budget. Because users travel with other people more often than they travel alone, users should have an effective way to share travel plans, ideas, and allow others to make changes.

After developing a series of research findings, the next step is to create a research report that will outline the research goals, methodology, findings, and recommendations. A research report is a necessary and valuable tool to present the research to teams and stakeholders in a digestible way, ensuring that the findings are integrated effectively into the next stages of the design.

Conclusion

Effectively analyzing the qualitative data gathered in your user research is a crucial component of the design process. By uncovering patterns, themes, and findings within qualitative data, you will have strong evidence and a clear direction to make successful user-centered decisions.

👉 How to Conduct User Surveys

Learn how to build a user survey, analyze the data, and avoid biases.

While user interviews are a great way to get insights into how individual users think about a design, process, or product, how do we find out what users think overall? For example, how often do people visit the airport to travel? Once a year? Every month? By conducting user surveys, we can get insights into the attitudes or preferences of a large number of users.

What is a User Survey?

User surveys are a UX research method where designers and researchers create questions for a group of participants to answer about a design, process, or product. Along with user interviews, they serve as a platform for users to freely express how they feel about a topic or product. A user survey can be used at many different points throughout the design process to check in with the users about how they feel when interacting with the design.

Correctly written surveys provide meaningful quantitative data. Quantitative data is information that can be measured, counted, or viewed as a numerical value. Measures of quantitative data answer the “what” of a problem. Looking at data from quantitative research methods makes it easier to notice common trends or patterns among users. Once you identify these trends and patterns, you can dig deeper into the problem and develop new solutions to solve user problems.

In this article, we will cover how to plan and conduct user surveys, analyze the data from surveys, and avoid survey biases.

Writing a Research Plan

Before we can send out a survey, we first need to develop a research plan. Writing a research plan is vital to ensure your user research is well-oriented and the research goal is clearly communicated to your team.

You can download the Research Plan Template to follow along with the process of writing out a research plan for your first user survey!

Planning a vacation takes time and effort - which can make it feel like the job you’re escaping from. Your team wants to build a platform to allow different people to share itineraries and recommendations when traveling to different cities or countries - to streamline the process of planning for a vacation.

  • What is the ideal way for people to plan their travels?
  • What are the tools people use to plan for a vacation?
  • Are people likely to collaborate on the process of creating travel plans?

For our example scenario, our target audience is people who enjoy traveling.

↪ Writing Survey Questions

Survey Structure

A user survey should include an introduction, survey questions, and a wrap-up.

A survey introduction should be included at the top of the survey to clearly state its purpose to the participants. It should include details about what the survey data will inform and how long the survey will take.

There should also be a wrap-up at the end of the survey to thank participants for their time and to let them know if you would be following up.

Survey Question Types

There are two types of survey questions: Closed-ended and open-ended questions.

Closed-ended questions are questions that can be answered by a yes or no, multiple choice, scales, or net promoter score questions. These questions allow you to be able to compare responses across participants and conduct a quantitative analysis to identify patterns.

Scales questions use Interval or Likert Scales to capture rating or polarity. Interval scales can be used to collect a numerical rating and are often used to find an average in how participants rank a certain topic. Likert scales can be used to gauge the level of polarity—how much do participants agree or disagree about a topic? Likert scales should offer the same number of positive and negative choices, and should always include a neutral option.

Open-ended questions ask participants to write out their responses in the form of free response questions. These questions generate qualitative data and are often used to get additional context about their responses from other closed-ended questions.

Writing Successful Survey Questions

Not all survey questions are created equal! It’s important to write clear and effective questions in order to collect relevant and unbiased data for your study.

Tip #1: Use simple words that your participants will easily understand. Using difficult or technical words will lower the engagement and prevent participants from accurately answering the questions or finishing the survey.

Example: In what way do you obtain your boarding pass before ingressing an aircraft?

The above example is a bad question, which could be reworded to “How do you access your boarding pass?” to simplify the language.

Tip #2: Avoid questions leading with bias. Biased scales occur when an unequal amount of positive or negative responses are provided, forcing or tempting the participants to answer a particular way. Keep an eye out for this one— this is easy to do in a survey accidentally.

Example: How likely are you to recommend our airline to a friend: 1 (Not at all likely), 2 (Not likely), 3 (Likely), 4 (Very likely), 5 (Extremely likely)?

This question is a biased scale because out of 5 choices, 3 choices lean on the likely side. There is no neutral option.

Tip #3: Avoid double-barreled questions. A double-barreled question asks the participant about two different problems in a single question.

Example: How would you rate navigation signs and security screening on a scale of 1 to 5?

This question is a double-barrelled question because it asks about two topics: navigation signs and security screening. What if a participant has no issues with navigation signs, but has big issues with security screening?

Example User Survey

Now that you understand how to prepare a user survey, let’s go back to our scenario and build a sample example survey.

Remember, our overarching research goal is to identify the process young adults use to plan for a vacation, where recommendations are found and evaluated, and how they feel about this process as a whole. Keeping that in mind and referring back to our research plan, let’s build a survey.

  • How frequently do users travel?


We’ll want to know how frequently users travel to compare whether frequent travelers (who travel 4+ a year) behave differently from infrequent travelers (who travel <4 trips a year). In this case, we’ll want to gather quantitative data in order to look at patterns across participants, so we’ll use a multiple choice question.

Note, if a user chooses the answer 0, we will want the user to exit the survey at this point. Because we are interested in the habits of traveling, we do not want to introduce data given by participants who are not part of the target audience. On Google Forms, you can show questions based on answers by choosing the option “Go to section based on answer” for the question and selecting “Submit form” for the specific answer.

  • How do users plan for vacations?


We’ll want to build some questions to help us understand how users plan for a vacation. We’ll start by asking the user about how much planning went into their most recent trip, to understand how much planning people do or whether they plan for trips at all.

If the participant chooses either of the answers that express that they planned for the trip in some capacity, we will then want to ask follow-up questions about their planning process. If the participant chooses the answer “I didn’t do any planning”, we’ll want them to skip to the next section of questions. On Google Forms, you can show questions based on answers by choosing the option “Go to section based on answer” for the question and selecting the correct section to skip to for the specific answer.

These questions will help us understand the resources people use. Since people might use many different resources or resources we have not considered, we’ll want to use checkboxes here and allow an option to write in an answer.

  • How do users collaborate in the planning process when traveling with others?


This section will help us compare whether solo travelers plan trips differently from group travelers. Referencing their most recent trip, the following question will ask participants who they traveled with.

If they select the answer “By myself”, the participant can skip the following section all together. Otherwise, we will want to ask further questions about the processes and tools they used to plan a trip with others. Like in the last section, these questions will provide context, so we can allow users to select multiple options or write in their own answers when needed.

  • Demographics and Conclusion


Finally, we will want to learn a little bit more about the characteristics of the participants. We will end the survey with a couple of demographic questions. It is good practice to ask demographic questions at the end for a couple of reasons. One, participants might be bored or tired by this point. Demographic questions are easier to answer. And, two, demographic questions can seem inquisitive. By this point, we have built trust with participants so they might feel more comfortable answering questions about themselves.


We’ll end the survey with a short conclusion thanking the participant for their time and detailing any next steps.

The survey is complete! Now it’s time to send it out to participants and gather the data.

↪ Data Analysis

Once you have stopped accepting responses to your survey, the next step is to organize and analyze your data in order to identify patterns, themes, and insights.

Raw data can be difficult to parse through—especially if there are a lot of responses. So before we can draw any information from the data, it’s important to organize and visualize it in a digestible way.

Charts

Charts are a quick and easy way to make your data easy to read and understand. Oftentimes, survey platforms such as Google Forms or Survey Monkey will automatically create charts from questions that produce quantitative data - choosing the one that fits best based on the format of the question.

  • Bar Charts

    Bar charts present categorical data using axes and bars to represent the measures. Bar charts are a good way to quickly compare the frequency of different responses between participants. From these frequencies, you can identify patterns and insights about common ideas, opinions, or behaviors.


  • Pie Charts

Pie charts represent the proportions of each type of response by visualizing these proportions as “slices” on a circle. Like bar graphs, pie charts can be useful to compare the frequency and significance of different responses and develop findings accordingly.




That being said, keep in mind that pie charts aren’t always the most human-centered way of visualizing data! It can be hard to easily compare the slices visually, especially if they are close in size. And if there are too many slices, it can be really hard to see any significance between them!

↪ Biases

Much like you want to avoid writing survey questions that lead to biased answers, you also want to make sure you’re not introducing bias as you analyze the results.

  • Confirmation Bias

Confirmation bias is the tendency to look at data and interpret it in a way that supports our opinions and beliefs. Since, as the researcher, you know exactly what the goal of the study is, you might make connections that aren’t explicit in the data set, based on your own opinions, beliefs, or expectations on how the goal should be met.

For example, here we see we have the question asked, “Which of the following emotions do you feel when at the airport? Please select the options below.”

Confirmation bias would be looking at this data and only reporting and developing findings based on the statistic “57.1 of our participants are excited when traveling at the airport.” While this is true, it ignores the other data and emotions, that 42.9% also feel anxious, confused, and frustrated.

  • Framing Effect

The framing effect occurs when insights or findings are presented in a way that is disingenuous. This might be by failing to report other aspects of the data, using statistical measures that are skewed, or wording the finding to imply a certain idea or opinion. You want to present the data in a way that accurately represents respondents’ opinions.

For example, let’s say 20 survey respondents rate their overall approval of a certain product on a Likert scale of 1 to 5. Take a look at the bar chart with the responses below.


You report that the average approval rate is between 2 and 3, meaning that users are slightly dissatisfied. Considering that the mean is 2.45, this is technically correct. But it is a disingenuous finding - only 25% of the users actually rated the product between a 2 and a 3.

Looking more closely at this data, it would be more accurate to report that the majority of users are either extremely dissatisfied or extremely satisfied with the product. By reporting the finding that the approval rate is between 2 and 3, your team might miss the root of the issue that causes disparities in the satisfaction of certain groups of users. This is an example of how framing the results can change the implications of the data.

Conclusion

User surveys are a valuable UX research tool to collect large amounts of data, discover patterns, and understand the perspective of the target audience as a whole. Following a user survey, you can then dive deeper into specific ideas or individual experiences by conducting user interviews.

A successful user survey relies on a defined research goal and plan, clear and well-written survey questions, and accurate data analysis and visualization. When conducted correctly, a survey gives users a chance to be honest about how they feel about your product, allowing you to develop a more human-centered design.

👉 How to Conduct Card Sorting

Learn what card sorting is and how to conduct your own in this step-by-step tutorial.

A good information architecture is crucial for a product. You want to easily find what you’re looking for and understand how to get there, right? Imagine you’re on a website shopping for a new toaster. There’s a home appliance category, and an electronics category. Which category will you check to find your new toaster?

Card sorting allows designers to organize information in a way that makes sense to users, with the help of users! In a card sorting activity, participants are asked to organize cards into groups that make the most sense to them. Card sorting is often used to improve the information architecture of a website or any platform.

After reading this article, you will thoroughly understand the importance of card sorting, its benefits, and how to conduct a card sorting session of your own!

What is Card Sorting?

Card sorting is a user research technique that requires participants to organize cards into groups. For this to happen, researchers are required to write down keywords on either paper or digital notecards. This method is often used during the early stages of the research phase and is frequently used for designing information architecture.

A card sorting session can take place during a one-on-one interview (moderated card sorting), or can be sent out to a large number of participants to complete in their own time (unmoderated card sorting). We’ll go into further details about moderated and unmoderated card sorting later!

↪ Open Card Sorting

Open card sorting is when participants are given a stack of cards with topics and asked to categorize them in any number of groups. Afterward, participants are asked to name each group.

↪ Closed Card Sorting

Closed card sorting is when researchers create labels for categories and ask participants to sort the individual cards into named categories. In closed card sorting, researchers discover how well the existing structure supports information architecture from a user’s point of view. During a closed card sort, researchers are evaluating if participants will place the cards into the expected category.

Now that we have discussed two types of card sorting, let’s move over to discussing different types of card sorting sessions: moderated and unmoderated sessions.

↪ Moderated Card Sorting

Moderated card sorting is when the researcher conducts a one-on-one interview with a participant to probe why the participant categorized the cards in one way or another. This type of session provides qualitative data because researchers can ask follow-up questions and receive insights from participants as to why they placed a specific card in a certain group.

↪ Unmoderated Card Sorting

Unmoderated card sorting requires participants to organize cards into their groups without a researcher overseeing the session, usually via an online tool. This method generates quantitative data and is good for conducting large-scale research. The researcher can send out the card sorting activity to many participants to complete in their own time.

One downside of unmoderated card sorting is that researchers are not able to ask follow-up questions, making it difficult to understand the reasoning behind why a participant placed a certain card in a category.

Pros and Cons of Card Sorting

As we know, card sorting is a very effective way of developing information architecture. But, similar to every user research technique, there are pros and cons.

↪ Pros

  • Card sorting is simple for participants to understand.
  • It is easy to conduct, whether you’re using physical or digital cards.
  • Moderated open card sorting allows us to receive insight into a user’s thought process by providing qualitative data.
  • Closed card sorting is a fast process that allows us to gain large amounts of data easily.

↪ Cons

  • Card sorting assumes topics on each card have equal importance.
  • When conducting closed card sort, the pre-created categories might not match what participants have in mind.
  • Participants may not have context about your product, which can lead to misalignment in expectations.
  • For example, let’s say you are looking to buy a baseball. If you are on a large retail website such as Amazon, you may just head over to the ‘Sports’ category to make your purchase. But if you are on a larger retail website such as Dick’s Sporting Goods, you may expect a more specific category name, like ‘Baseball’.

If you want to conduct a closed card sort, ensure you have categorized sections.

  1. Participants Organize Cards into Groups


    Ask your participants to group the cards that are closely related.

👉 Writing a Research Report

Learn how to gather user research and combine it into the form of a research report.

Let’s say, a few weeks ago, you conducted a few user interviews. You’ve gathered all your research insights and data and would like a way to organize your thoughts, present new findings, and showcase your hard work to your team or organization.

What you need is a research report! Creating a research report can be done in many ways to properly communicate your findings to your audience.

What should the research report contain? Do you want it to be a written document or a presentation with visuals?

This article will help you understand how to write a clear and concise research report and present those new findings to your audience.

What is a Research Report?

A research report is a UX research deliverable that summarizes insights from a single user research study. It can be in various forms, such as a written report or a slide deck.

The report should tell a story about the users and share research findings across your organization. It should be digestible and help designers and stakeholders make big decisions about the next steps to take in the project or design.

Before creating a research report, you should ask yourself a few questions. Who is the audience of the report? What are the key takeaways you want the audience to remember? To decide what to include, we first need to thoroughly understand what components go inside a research report.

Research Report Formats

Research reports typically come in two formats: a written report and presentation slides.

↪ Presentation Report

A slide presentation is one of the more popular ways to create a research report. The information about the research and findings are divided into slides. For example, one slide would discuss the executive summary, the next would discuss the background, and so on. We will go into the essential components of a research report in the next section.

Presentation reports are flexible and engaging, allowing you to tell a story with your research and include illustrations or visual data. They’re easier to navigate and easier to visually highlight deliverables and data.

↪ Written Report

A written research report is a written document that includes all user research and findings. It includes the same essential components as a presentation report, but instead of including main findings and summarized insights on a slide, a written report would aim to go into further depth. With that said, it can be lengthier than a presentation report.

Frequently, stakeholders or other team members do not have time to read written research reports and may have trouble processing a huge wall of text. They may skip over valuable information or not thoroughly understand the importance of key findings. Additionally, they can be time-consuming to write and usually are less engaging than a slide presentation.

The format of the research report you want to create is up to you and your team. However, it is important to consider the audience, time, and how you want to communicate your research to your audience.

The Essential Components

What can go in a research report can vary, but there are some essential components you should include.

Take a moment to look at our research report template. We will go over the different components in the research report template together.

A research report typically contains the following sections:

  • Executive Summary
  • Background
  • Research Method
  • Research Questions
  • Findings
  • Recommendations
  • What’s Next

↪ Executive Summary

An executive summary is an overview included at the beginning of the research report that summarizes the key insights and the purpose of the study. The executive summary should be concise and be able to stand on its own. Always include an executive summary in your report. Think of it as your introduction or hook to engage your audience!

↪ Background

The background should include the what and the why of the project. This section of the report should include the context, discuss why you began research in the first place, and the problem you are trying to solve. This could be a one-sentence statement from a previous study, stating the problem that prompted you to conduct the current study you will be discussing. For example, “Our industry reports stated that in 2021 70% of young adults in the U.S. have trouble planning for a vacation and findings recommendations on places to visit” highlights a problem and lets the audience know the context, target audience, and what problem you are trying to solve.

↪ Research Questions

The research questions should come directly from your research plan. For example, the research questions from the example scenario in the How to Conduct User Interviews article were:

  • How do young adults plan for a vacation?
  • What tools do they use?
  • When traveling with others, how do young adults collaborate on traveling planning? Including research questions in the research report gives your audience context about how you began this project, and that the report will be centered around answering these questions.

↪ Research Method

In this section, you should discuss what user research methods you have used and why. This section should also include the number of participants in the study and the demographic / target audience. Explain how you recruited your participants, held interviews or usability testing sessions, and analyzed results. For example, this could be as simple as stating, “we emailed surveys to 500 customers and received 200 responses.”

↪ Findings

Findings are a big part of the research report as they summarize what you’ve learned from conducting this study. This could be accompanied by a quote from a user interview or a graph from a user survey. What are the essential insights that you want the audience to take away? Findings can be referenced in a research report more than once. For example, you may want to include 2-3 key findings in your executive summary. If you plan to suggest recommendations in the research report, you may want to pair a recommendation with a key finding.

↪ Recommendations

Consider this part your call to action. What do you want to do to solve this problem for users? What ideas do you and your teammates have to make a service, product, or process better for users? For example, this could be a design improvement on a digital service such as fixing a blocker to prevent a user from completing an important task within a platform. Suppose your recommendation so happens to be a design improvement. In that case, it may be helpful to include a quick sketch.

↪ What’s Next

The name nearly speaks for itself! The “What’s Next?” slide should include the next steps for your research study. In this slide, briefly explain what you would like to do after the research is done, and why. An example of this could be creating a user persona to enhance product marketing, or conducting a quantitative study to validate user data that has been observed from previous studies.

Research Report Best Practices

Telling an engaging story about the users should be a goal of your research report. Here are some helpful tips to do this!

↪ Make Information Digestible

Be brief yet effective with your writing. Stakeholders may not have the time to read lots of information. Similar to a user survey, the words in a research report should be easy to comprehend for the report to be effective.

Analyzing raw data can be challenging. To make information digestible and understandable for your audience, it is helpful to provide visual deliverables such as graphs, charts, timelines, and illustrations that are directly related to your report.

If you are presenting the research report, consider having flashcards or notes and practice what you want to discuss. Your slides should contain a summary of the information and go into greater detail when speaking to your audience.

↪ Consider The Audience

A good research report considers its audience. Making sure your presentation meets the needs of your audience would be an effective way to accomplish this. It is helpful to keep that in mind as you decide how to present data and insights, and what data or findings from your research you want to include.

👉 Creating Personas

An overview of what personas are, how they support the design process, and how to create them.

Imagine you’re designing a new product that will improve the process of planning a trip. To start this project, you conducted interviews and surveys to learn more about your target audience: people who like to travel.

After analyzing the research, you discovered insights about what users think and do when they plan their trips. This information provides a more clear, detailed, and complex picture of who the users are. After all, “people who like to travel” is a broad category that includes users with a variety of goals and mindsets.

At this point, you’re looking for a way to summarize what you know about the users. You want to make the data memorable and actionable. Additionally, you want to effectively communicate your findings with team members and stakeholders. This is where personas come in!

What Is a Persona?

A persona is a fictional archetype of the target users of a product or design, created as a composite of user research or market research.

While personas are fictional, we use real data to create them. We say “fictional” because the persona does not represent one specific person. Instead, each persona represents data from a subset of many users who have something in common, such as shared goals or behaviors.

Broadly speaking, personas define the following about our users:

  • Who are they? (behaviors, mindsets, demographics)
  • What is their end goal?
  • Why might they use the product?

Defining users’ key characteristics sets us up to create a product that they will actually find useful. By organizing these details into a persona, we create a succinct document that we can easily reference throughout the design process.

↪ Persona vs. User Role

User roles define different types of users who will interact with the product. In comparison, personas provide more in-depth information about who users are and what they need from the product.

For example, let’s say we’re making a travel website that features locals’ reviews of food, lodging, and attractions. For this website, there are probably two distinct user roles:

  • Locals (post reviews)
  • Travelers (read and comment on reviews)

Defining the roles of “local” and “traveler” is helpful, but it doesn’t give us much information about who the users are. At this point, we could conduct user research about both types of users. Then, we would create personas for each role.

Why Use Personas?

After conducting user research, you may create many artifacts to organize the data and communicate your findings. If you already plan to make an affinity map and a research report, why would you need personas?

↪ Make Research Personal

When it comes to data organization, personas provide a unique value: they make user research feel personal. As we build personas, we shape the data into something that resembles a profile or even a character.

This “profile format” helps us envision a human being rather than a collection of data points. When we can visualize a person, it becomes easier to empathize with the end users. Additionally, when we can make a personal connection to the data, it’s often easier to remember.

↪ Establish a Shared Understanding

When designing, we typically collaborate with multiple team members and stakeholders. As we work together, we must align on core aspects of the design, such as:

  • What problem will this design attempt to solve?
  • Who are we designing for?

Problem statements help us with the first question — personas help us with the second.

We should keep in mind that not every team member has the opportunity to conduct user research. In the absence of data, each person may reach their own conclusions about who the target users are. Without a shared vision, it could be difficult to make decisions or create a user-centered design.

We can leverage personas to communicate our research findings. Personas are designed to be succinct and digestible, with a focus on the most impactful details. Thus, personas are an effective tool for helping teams align on their target users.

What’s Included in a Persona?

Depending on the project, the content and level of detail in a persona will vary. Sometimes, we may craft brief personas with just a few details. In other situations, we may create detailed personas that provide more thorough insights.

↪ Common Elements

  • Name & Title
  • Photograph or Illustration
  • Demographic information
  • Goals
  • Mindsets & Behaviors
  • Scenario

↪ Name & Title

The persona’s name provides a personal touch and makes it easy to reference them in discussions. The name should be fictional and shouldn’t match any of your participants.

The title encapsulates the persona’s core goal or behavior, which helps us remember their defining characteristics.


Example: For the travel app, each persona’s title correlates to their behaviors around travel planning. “Casual Wanderer” Cat plans a few things in advance but prefers to get recommendations from the people she meets while traveling. “Detailed Planner” Lennox invests substantial time before a trip to research recommendations.

↪ Photograph or Illustration

We can include a photograph or illustration to put a face to the name. This is another technique for humanizing the persona.

If using photographs, we should stick to stock photos. We want to avoid using personal photos or celebrity photos, as this can lead to bias or distract from the content. Unsplash and Pexels offer free stock photos, and This Person Does Not Exist generates faces of fictional people.

↪ Demographic Information

Demographic information refers to biographical details such as age, gender, location, occupation, education, or experience with the product. Like a name and photograph, these details can bring the persona to life.

When deciding what demographic information to include, we should consider

  • Do we have the research to back this up?
  • Does this information have an impact on how we will design the product?

↪ Goals

The goals describe what the persona needs or wants to get done when they interact with the product. In general, users’ end goal is not to use a product! Instead, they’re using the product to accomplish a task or solve a problem.

Additionally, we should only include goals that are relevant to our design. During the interview, a participant might share their personal goal of renovating their childhood home. Unless this is relevant to the problem space, we shouldn’t add it to the persona.

Example: (Lennox, Detailed Planner): Discover excellent travel recommendations before the trip, create itineraries to use during the trip, and communicate details with the group so everyone has the information they need

↪ Behaviors & Mindset

Behaviors include what the user does, and mindset covers how the user thinks or feels. Mindset can be broken down into more specific categories like attitudes, beliefs, frustrations, and motivations. We should only include behaviors and mindsets that connect to the context of our product.

Example Behaviours: (Lennox, Detailed Planner): Researches travel recommendations using multiple sources such as friends/travel blogs/travel apps, conducts thorough research before the trip, and stores information in a spreadsheet.

Example Mindset: (Lennox, Detailed Planner): Excited about finding the best recommendations — wherever those may come from, doesn't mind planning and likes following a set planning process, and feels it's important to get input from all travel partners before the trip.

↪ Scenario

The scenario explains when and why the persona might interact with the product. We want to provide context that explores how the product fits into the persona’s life. The scenario may address:

  • When will they use the product?
  • How frequently will they use the product?
  • What problem(s) does the product help them solve?
  • What environment(s) will they be in when using the product?

The scenario is typically a few sentences and is written from the persona’s point of view.

Example: (Lennox, Detailed Planner): I have a trip coming up in four weeks! I looked on three travel blogs and connected with friends who lived in or visited the destination. I have all my recommendations saved. Now, I need a way to share this with my travel companions. In the past, they have not opened the spreadsheet because it was inconvenient.

↪ A Note on Avoiding Bias

When including certain persona elements, such as demographic details and photographs, it’s possible for implicit bias to creep in. We may hold assumptions about how a demographic group thinks or behaves. These assumptions detract from our goal of defining who users are (not who we think they are).

For example, if our persona is a teenager, we shouldn’t assume that they are tech-savvy. Teenagers are a diverse group that encompasses many backgrounds and experiences. If the persona will feature technology experience, that information should come from our user research.

One approach to mitigating bias in personas is to limit the demographic information. In addition to possibly creating bias, demographic information is not usually the most impactful part of the persona. More often than not, the persona’s goals, behaviors, and mindset will have more influence over our design. To learn more about de-emphasizing the role of demographic details in personas, check out Maze’s resource on personas.

How Can We Create Personas?

Personas are typically created during the “define” stage of the design thinking process. After conducting research in the “empathize” stage, we have the information needed to identify key characteristics of our users.

Let’s say that we conducted user research that yielded a mix of quantitative and qualitative data. After reviewing the information, we used an affinity map to organize our findings.

Now that we’re familiar with the data, we’re ready to create personas.

  1. Look for Patterns in the Data

    We can start by identifying patterns in users’ goals and behaviors:
    - What are users trying to accomplish?
    - What are their actions, habits, and routines?

    While we’ll eventually dig into other elements, goals and behaviors are often a good starting point for defining our personas. For the travel app example, we could look for patterns in what users want to accomplish when they plan their trips (goals). Then, we might examine the actions that they take while planning (behaviors).
  2. Determine How Many Personas to Create

A product may have one or more personas. As a general rule, we don’t want too many personas because they can blur together. We can start by looking for major differences between users’ goals or behaviors. If there are significant differences, we can create one persona for each point of view (about 2-4 personas total).

However, the data may not reveal any compelling reasons to create multiple personas. We can always start with one persona!

For the travel app example, user research indicates that some users plan in detail prior to a trip while others limit their advanced planning. This is a notable distinction in how users may interact with the product, so we can create two personas.

  1. Include Defining Characteristics

Once we have a general overview of each persona, we can elaborate on their key characteristics. If we use goals and behaviors as the foundation, then we can add details about mindset, such as frustrations, attitudes, and motivations. We might find a quote or two from the interview that conveys the persona’s core essence.

We should only include information that comes from our research, and all details should be relevant to the context of our design.

  1. Create a Scenario


Now that we have clear descriptions of our personas, we can write scenarios. The scenarios clarify the context in which the persona will use our product.

The scenario should be based on data and written from the persona’s point of view.

  1. Add Personal Touches

Bring the persona to life with demographic information, a name and title, and a photograph/illustration.

By waiting to add personal details, we can mitigate implicit bias. This way, we avoid building a persona based on our perception of demographic information or a photograph.

  1. Choose a Format

There are many different persona formats we can use! You can find free persona templates online, or by looking for resources in Miro or the Figma Community. When using a premade template, customize it to fit your project. Omit or revise sections that aren’t relevant to your user research or design.

You can also create persona templates from scratch with your design tool of choice. Overall, the goal is to display the information clearly, so that it’s easy for people to review the document and scan for specific details. It doesn’t need to look fancy in order to be useful! In fact, you may even try a list-style persona if you want to keep it simple.

↪ Additional Considerations

  • Portray the persona respectfully: While personas are fictional, they represent real people. Thus, we should avoid using language that invites judgment. Depicting the persona in a negative light can chip away at the empathy we are trying to build with our users. For example, if a persona prefers to book discounted flights and lodging, we don’t want to call them cheap. Instead, we can describe them with a neutral term, such as budget-conscious.
  • Iterate as needed: In order to be useful to the design process, personas must be accurate and up-to-date. If new user research is conducted, we should revisit the personas and make any necessary changes. Alternately, the product may change significantly over time. Depending on these changes, we may need to remove, revise, or add personas.
  • Put them to use: After conducting research and carefully crafting a persona, we want to put them to good use! To get the most value from our personas, we must consistently reference them as we design. Once your team is familiar with the personas, the persona names can serve as a shortcut for putting certain user needs into focus. You might say, “How would this affect Cat? What about Lennox? Which persona are we prioritizing?”

Conclusion

By creating personas, we create an artifact that equips us to follow a user-centered design process. To get the most value out of personas:

  • Base the details on user research
  • Focus on information that is relevant to the problem space
  • Reference personas consistently during the design process
  • Update the personas as new information is revealed
